# Presentation Zen

Presentation Zen is a philosophy, not a formula. Three principles guide
everything:

1. **Restraint in preparation** — Find the one core message. Cut the rest.
2. **Simplicity in design** — Maximum effect with minimum means.
3. **Naturalness in delivery** — Be fully present. Connect like a human, not a performer.

The model: a Japanese bento box. Beautiful, balanced, nothing superfluous.
Everything present is there for a reason. Nothing lacking, nothing excess.

---

## Phase 1: Preparation — Go Analog First

Before opening any software, step away from the computer. The biggest mistake
presenters make is building slides before clarifying thinking.

### Find the core message

Ask these before anything else:

- **What is my point?** One sentence.
- **Why does it matter?** (Japanese: _Dakara nani?_ — "So what?")
- **If the audience remembers ONE thing, what should it be?**

Run the **elevator test**: Can you deliver the core message in 30–45 seconds?
If not, the message is not clear enough yet.

### Questions to answer before designing

- How much time do I have? What is the venue like?
- Who is the audience? What is their background? What do they expect?
- Why was I asked to speak? What do I want them to _do_?
- What is the fundamental purpose of this talk?

### The 5-step analog process

1. **Brainstorm** — Paper, sticky notes, whiteboard. No computer. Quantity over
   quality here. Write every idea. Do not judge yet.
2. **Group and identify core** — Chunk similar ideas. Find the unifying theme.
   Aim for 3 sections (a manageable constraint, not a rule).
3. **Storyboard off the computer** — Arrange sticky notes in sequence. See the
   flow before building slides.
4. **Sketch visuals** — Rough drawings of what images, charts, or diagrams will
   appear. Not art — just enough to visualize each slide's purpose.
5. **Build in slideware** — Only now, open Keynote/PowerPoint. Build from your
   storyboard, not from scratch.

### Slides vs. documents: never merge them

Slides are slides. Documents are documents. The "slideument" — slides stuffed
with text that must also serve as a leave-behind — is the enemy of both.

- **Slides**: visual, minimal, support your spoken words
- **Handout**: a separate written document with full depth and detail
- **Notes**: for your rehearsal, not for the audience

Never distribute printed slides as a handout. If the slides can stand alone
without you, you are unnecessary. If they cannot stand alone, printing them
is useless.

## Phase 2: Crafting the Story — Make It Stick

Data and bullet points are forgettable. Stories are not.

### The SUCCESs framework (from _Made to Stick_)

Memorable ideas share these qualities:

| Principle          | What it means                                                                                |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **S**implicity     | One core idea. Ruthlessly cut everything else. If everything is important, nothing is.       |
| **U**nexpectedness | Violate expectations. Open a knowledge gap, then fill it. Take them on a journey.            |
| **C**oncreteness   | Real images, real things, real language. Not "maximize synergies" — "put a man on the moon." |
| **C**redibility    | Establish trust through vivid examples and meaningful context, not company history slides.   |
| **E**motions       | Make them _feel_ something. Images trigger emotion; bullet points don't.                     |
| **S**tories        | Frame everything as narrative. Problem → conflict → resolution.                              |

### Story structure

Every great presentation has conflict at its core:

1. **Identify the problem** — What pain exists?
2. **Show the conflict** — Real examples, causes, opposing forces
3. **Resolve the conflict** — How and why you solved it

Look for contrasts: before/after, problem/solution, past/future, decline/growth.
Contrasts are inherently dramatic. They create narrative tension.

### What makes stories memorable

- Emotional content (surprise, empathy, curiosity) anchors memory
- Human terms beat abstractions ("what 100 grams of fat _looks like_" beats "100 grams of fat")
- Authenticity — internalize the story, don't memorize it line by line. You can't fake belief.
- Conversational voice — our brains treat conversational narrative like a dialogue we're part of

### Editing is the job

After drafting: edit like George Lucas cutting _Star Wars_ to two hours. Every
scene either serves the story or gets cut. Ask of every element:

- Does this support my core message?
- Would removing it lose the audience anything essential?

**When in doubt, cut it out.** Nobody ever got fired for including too much —
but audiences remember presenters who respect their time.

## Phase 3: Design — Simplicity as Clarity

### The Zen aesthetic

Three Japanese concepts for slide design:

- **Kanso** (簡素) — Simplicity. Maximum effect with minimum elements.
- **Shizen** (自然) — Naturalness. No elaborate ornamentation. Restraint is beautiful.
- **Shibumi** (渋み) — Understated elegance. "Less is more" as the highest taste.

Eliminate until you can eliminate no more without losing the message.
Then stop. (Einstein: "Everything should be made as simple as possible, but not simpler.")

### Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)

Every element on a slide is either signal (relevant) or noise (irrelevant).
Maximize signal. Minimize noise.

Remove or minimize:

- Company logos on every slide (use only first and last)
- Decorative borders, backgrounds, gradients
- Footnotes and legal boilerplate (move to handout)
- 3D effects on 2D data
- Animation that doesn't serve the message
- Clip art and stock clichés (hands shaking in front of a globe = put the pen down)

### Picture superiority effect

Images are remembered far better than words after 30+ seconds.
Use photographs, diagrams, and visual metaphors instead of describing things in text.
When you show a powerful image and speak the words, both channels reinforce each other.

### Bullet points

Use bullets **rarely**, only after considering every alternative. Ask:

- Can I replace this list with a single powerful image?
- Can I replace this list with one clear sentence I say aloud?
- Can I replace this list with a diagram or chart?

Bullet point walls prevent connection. The audience reads instead of listening.
You become narration for a document they could have received by email.

### The "big four" design principles (CRAP)

- **Contrast** — Make different things look different. Clear visual hierarchy.
- **Repetition** — Consistent fonts, colors, and styles create coherence.
- **Alignment** — Nothing placed arbitrarily. Every element has a visual relationship.
- **Proximity** — Related items grouped. Unrelated items separated.

### Empty space is a tool

White space (or negative space) is not wasted space — it's breathing room.
Empty space directs attention to what remains. A single image on a white field
is more powerful than that image crowded by bullets.

The Japanese "one-corner" style of painting: a vast ocean with a tiny canoe in
the corner. The emptiness _creates_ the vastness.

### Practical slide guidelines

- One idea per slide (you can use many slides — the number doesn't matter)
- Full-bleed photographs that fill the frame
- Large, readable type (if you need a pointer to read it, the slide has failed)
- 2D charts for 2D data
- Limit color palette (2–3 colors maximum for consistency)
- No slide templates with built-in bullet fields — start from blank

## Phase 4: Delivery — Be Completely Present

### The "no mind" principle

Like a master swordsman, once technique is internalized through practice, stop
_thinking_ about technique. The moment you think "am I doing this right?" you
have broken the connection.

Prepare and rehearse obsessively. Then, on stage, let go of the preparation
and be only in this moment with this audience.

### Full presence

- Your mind should not be racing through concerns, future slides, or past mistakes
- Do not read from notes or slides — internalize the material
- Mistakes happen; acknowledge and move on. Ben Zander's rule: "How fascinating!"
- Adjust in real time based on what the audience needs, not what you planned

### Connecting with the audience

The presenter is the presentation. Slides are support. You are the story.

- **Start with PUNCH**: Personal, Unexpected, Novel, Challenging, or Humorous
- Make the opening land before explaining who you are or why you're qualified
- Speak conversationally — as if having a real dialogue, not delivering a speech
- Use eye contact to create individual connections across the room
- Let passion and energy show physically — "one-buttock player" (lean in, move, commit)
- Humor is a genuine connector, not decoration — if something is funny, use it

### The contribution mindset

Ask not "will they like me?" but "what can I contribute?" You are the gift.
Your knowledge and perspective are what they came for. This shift removes
performance anxiety and replaces it with genuine generosity.

### Handling difficulty

- Hostile questions: receive without defensiveness, respond without butting heads
- Technical failures: shrug and continue. If you know the material, you don't need the slides.
- Going over time: knowing when to stop is wisdom. Respect the audience's time above your content.

### Practice plan

- Rehearse aloud, full speed, multiple times
- Know the material well enough to present without any slides
- Practice until it feels boring to you — then it will feel natural to them

## Quick Reference

**Before you open any software:**

- [ ] Core message in one sentence
- [ ] "So what?" answered clearly
- [ ] Audience profile understood
- [ ] Analog brainstorm done (paper/whiteboard)
- [ ] Storyboard sketched

**Slide design check:**

- [ ] One idea per slide
- [ ] Images over bullet text
- [ ] Noise removed (logos, decorations, unnecessary text)
- [ ] Handout created separately
- [ ] Slides cannot stand alone without the speaker (good)

**Delivery check:**

- [ ] Rehearsed aloud multiple times
- [ ] Strong PUNCH opening ready
- [ ] Not planning to read from slides
- [ ] Knows what to say if projector dies

## Common Anti-Patterns to Fix

| Anti-pattern                                      | Fix                                              |
| ------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Slides full of bullets                            | One image + your spoken words                    |
| Reading slides aloud                              | Know the material; slides support, not replace   |
| Logo on every slide                               | Logo on first and last slide only                |
| Distributing slide printouts                      | Write a proper handout document                  |
| Starting with "Hi I'm X, today I'll talk about Y" | Start with the PUNCH — hook them first           |
| 3D pie charts                                     | Simple 2D bar chart                              |
| Template gradient backgrounds                     | Clean, minimal backgrounds                       |
| Too many slides with too much text                | More slides with less per slide is fine          |
| Explaining everything verbally AND visually       | Choose one channel for each piece of information |
| Spending prep time in PowerPoint                  | Go analog first, build slides last               |
